# Mesoamerican Sculpture and Relief Carving Reference

## Overview

Mesoamerican sculpture encompasses a vast range of media, scales, and purposes — from monumental 40-ton basalt heads to thumb-sized jade celts. The common thread is **symbolic density** and **material significance**: the choice of stone material carries meaning (jade = life/preciousness, obsidian = night/sacrifice, basalt = endurance/permanence). Relief carving is the primary medium for narrative and text, while in-the-round sculpture tends toward iconic/hieratic representation.

## Relief Carving Traditions

### Maya Stone Relief (Stelae, Lintels, Panels)

**Stelae**
- Upright stone slabs, typically 2–6 m tall and 1 m wide
- **Front face:** Ruler in full ritual regalia; often holding a Serpent Bar or staff; stood in a fixed iconic pose
- **Back and side faces:** Hieroglyphic text recording dates, events, and genealogy
- **Composition rules:**
  - Figure is centered vertically
  - Ruler's head is at the upper 60% of the composition (scale = importance)
  - Background filled with secondary glyphs or scroll motifs (horror vacui in Classic period)
  - Border: glyph band running around all four sides creates a framing "cartouche" for the entire composition
  - Ground line: absent or minimal; figure appears to float slightly

**Depth and Technique**
- **Shallow but layered:** Maya limestone allows very shallow relief with intricate internal detailing
- **Modeled surface:** Rounded forms, not flat planes; subtle depth changes create movement
- **Undercutting:** Selective undercutting creates sharp shadow lines at important contours
- **Scale hierarchy:** Main figure largest; secondary figures smaller; deity/ancestor in vision-serpent mouth smallest

**Lintels (Yaxchilan, Palenque)**
- Carved stone beams across doorways; horizontal format
- Among the finest Maya relief works; Yaxchilan lintels (6, 24, 25, 26): scenes of bloodletting ritual
- Horizontal composition: multiple figures; ground line provided by doorway threshold below
- Very high-quality carving with strong undercutting and fluid lines

**Palace Panels**
- Palenque Palace Tablet, Temple of Inscriptions panels: rectangular slabs
- Complex multi-figure compositions with flanking text columns
- Three-column layout: left text column | central image | right text column

### Aztec Monumental Sculpture

**Cult Statues and Cosmological Monuments**

*Coatlicue (National Anthropology Museum, Mexico City)*
- Height: 2.57 m; Weight: ~12 tonnes; andesite
- Frontal, upright, fully symmetric
- Head: two facing serpent heads forming a face; no human face
- Necklace: severed hands + hearts + skull pendant
- Skirt: interlocked live serpents
- Feet: eagle claws gripping the earth
- Surface: entirely covered with symbolic elements; not a single plain surface area
- Original: painted; pigment traces survived

*Coyolxauhqui Disk*
- Circular; 3.25 m diameter; found at base of Templo Mayor
- Shows dismembered body of moon goddess
- Radial composition: head at 12 o'clock; limbs radiate; all elements fill the circle
- Body parts: arms, legs, torso, head — each severed, flowing with blood scrolls, wrapped in costume
- High relief with deep undercutting; originally painted

*Sun Stone (Aztec Calendar Stone)*
- 3.58 m diameter; 24 tonnes; andesite
- Concentric ring composition (see symbols/calendars.json for full breakdown)
- Intended for a horizontal surface (the face looks up at the sky)

*Tlaltecuhtli Block*
- 4.0 m × 3.6 m; discovered 2006
- Earth-monster deity; massive crouching form with clawed joints; each claw biting or grasping
- Underside of the Sun Stone (facing the earth)

**Sacrificial Vessels (Cuauhxicalli)**
- Eagle bowls and jaguar bowls used for heart/blood offerings
- Circular or shallow forms; often with central depression for offerings
- Decorated with solar or earth motifs on exterior

**Warrior Orders and Elite Sculpture**
- Eagle warrior and jaguar warrior ceramic life-size figures at Templo Mayor
- Ceramic; life-size; painted; installed in ceremonial halls (House of Eagles)

### Olmec Monumental Sculpture

**Colossal Heads** (see cultures/olmec.md for detail)
- 17 confirmed examples; San Lorenzo (9+), La Venta (4), Tres Zapotes (2), Rancho La Cobata (1)
- Scale: 1.4 m to 3.4 m tall; 6–40 tonnes
- Carved from basalt boulders; transported 50–100 km from Tuxtla Mountains
- Form: spherical with flattened back; features pressed forward; individualized faces
- Helmets: each has a distinct helmet form identifying the individual lord
- Surface: originally painted; weathered to natural basalt grey

**Thrones / Altars**
- 17 known; massive rectangular basalt blocks (Altar 4 at La Venta is 2.4 m wide)
- Front face: carved ruler figure emerging from a cave or mouth niche; holding a were-jaguar baby
- Side panels: procession of seated figures each holding a baby
- Interpretation: ruler as cosmic intermediary; cave = womb of the earth

**Stelae at La Venta**
- Upright flat stone slabs; La Venta Stela 3: two large standing figures with elaborate headdresses
- Incised rather than deeply carved; flat-relief style
- Second style from San Lorenzo: more rounded heads

### Teotihuacan Sculpture

**Feathered Serpent Pyramid Heads (Tenons)**
- Projecting serpent heads with feathered collars; alternating with storm deity (Tlaloc-related) heads
- Tenon style: head projects outward horizontally from the pyramid face
- Deep undercutting around the head; head appears to emerge from the wall
- Original: painted with jade green, cinnabar red, black; shell eye inlays

**Tlaloc Mask Facade**
- At certain Teotihuacan structures: large modeled Tlaloc face in stucco or stone on upper facade
- Simplified frontal face; goggle eyes as circles; fanged jaw

**Greenstone Funerary Masks**
- Flat frontal jade or greenstone masks; human face
- Found in elite burials beneath pyramids
- Large shell, obsidian, or pyrite eye inlays; open mouth
- Proportions: slightly wider than tall; features idealized; not portraits

---

### Zapotec Sculpture

**Danzante Reliefs**
- Carved into flat stone slabs embedded in platform walls
- "Danzantes" (dancers): figures in contorted positions; some with mutilation markings (scroll eyes = blood); glyph labels
- Period: Monte Albán I (~500–200 BCE); earliest major sculpture at site
- Style: simplified, flat figures; limited depth; strong outline

**Stucco Deity Heads (Lambityeco)**
- Life-size modeled stucco heads of Cocijo rain deity flanking tomb stairways
- Strong three-dimensional relief; thick neck; simplified but powerful facial features

## Technical Notes for Generative AI

### Relief Depth Conventions by Culture

| Culture        | Typical Relief Depth | Undercutting    | Edge Quality              |
|----------------|---------------------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| Maya Classic   | Shallow (1–3 cm)     | Selective, sharp| Smooth curves; fluid      |
| Aztec          | Medium (2–8 cm)      | Deep at key points | Hard angles; bold forms |
| Olmec          | In-the-round or deep | Very deep       | Smooth, rounded masses    |
| Teotihuacan    | Shallow-medium       | Minimal         | Clean geometric edges     |
| Zapotec        | Shallow–medium       | Minimal         | Simple, blocky lines      |
| Veracruz       | Medium               | Moderate        | Scroll curves; flowing    |

### Lighting for Relief

- **Raking light (low-angle):** Standard for ancient site conditions; sun at ~20–30° angle; reveals maximum relief depth
- **Channel shadows:** Deep carved channels are always darkest; pigment accumulates; shadow fills
- **Highlight edges:** Raised ridge tops catch brightest light; often slightly worn smooth by touch/weathering
- **Ambiguity:** Shallow relief requires light to read; at wrong angle, a composition can appear almost flat

### Weathering and Patina

- **Limestone:** Develops grey-brown patina; surface details erode from edges inward; rounding of sharp angles over centuries
- **Basalt:** More resistant; retains detail longer; develops thin dark surface oxidation film
- **Jade/greenstone:** Exceptionally resistant to weathering; retains surface quality; color remains vivid
- **Stucco:** Flakes and peels from stone core; survives best in protected recesses; pigment traces survive in deepest channels

## Compositional Conventions

### Frontal vs. Profile
- **Iconic/divine** = frontal face; the direct gaze = supernatural power looking out at viewer
- **Human/historical** = profile; the figure acts within the composition, not addressing the viewer
- **Mixed (Teotihuacan standard):** Body frontal, arms in action; resolves the iconic+narrative tension

### Scale Hierarchy (Vertical Scale = Social Rank)
- Primary figure (ruler, deity): occupies center and largest size
- Secondary figures (attendants, supplicants): smaller, flanking, lower
- Defeated captives: smallest, most degraded; sometimes shown upside-down or in painful postures
- Text/glyphs: smallest elements; fill remaining space

### Frame and Ground
- Aztec compositions: figures may float against flat ground; or ground is filled with symbolic detail
- Maya compositions: full horror vacui; every background space filled with secondary glyphs or scrolls
- Teotihuacan: strict register bands; background filled within band but not outside
- Olmec: plain ground; the form itself is the entire content

## Generative AI Directives

- **Maya relief:** Shallow, fluid, rounded forms; imagine modeling clay with a rounded wooden tool — no hard 90° corners; horror vacui backgrounds
- **Aztec relief:** Bolder, deeper, more architectural; imagine cutting with a firm chisel; forms are blockier; corners can be sharp; symbolic density
- **Olmec sculpture:** Think in 3D mass, not surface pattern; smooth spherical forms; the head is a sphere; features are gentle depressions and rises in a smooth surface
- **For lighting:** Always use raking directional light (not ambient); 15–30° angle from one side; this is how ancient carvings were designed to be seen
- **Material affects form:** Jade is carved to preserve material (compact, no thin extensions); basalt is rough and sturdy (no delicate details); limestone allows fine carving (Maya lintels)
- **Color in recesses:** The most common rendering insight — push dark pigment (red, cinnabar, black) into the deepest channels; raised surfaces are the stone color; produces dramatic contrast
- **Scale relationships:** Always establish the human figure scale reference before rendering any sculpture; a 3 m stela is extremely tall; a colossal head is 2–3 human heads in width
