# Mesoamerican Ceramics and Pottery Reference

## Overview

Mesoamerican ceramics serve ritual, funerary, storage, and domestic purposes. Each culture developed distinct ceramic traditions, ranging from highly painted narrative vessels to plain utilitarian wares. This document covers the major traditions relevant to generative visual work.

---

## Aztec (Mexica) Ceramics

### Aztec I–IV Black-on-Orange Ware
- **Period:** ~1200–1521 CE (progressive phases)
- **Form:** Bowls, tripod plates, jars
- **Surface:** Orange-red clay body; black painted geometric or figural decoration
- **Patterns:** Step-fret meander, checkerboard, interlocked scrolls, stylized animals
- **Aztec III (peak):** Black step-fret bands against orange; highly geometric; crisp, well-fired
- **Aztec IV:** Just before conquest; degenerate/simplified versions

*Generative notes:* Orange ground (#D46A30) with black geometric motifs; crisp, hard edges; step-fret and checker dominant; no figurative narrative in painted decoration (unlike Maya); flat and graphic

### Mixteca-Puebla Polychrome
- **Distribution:** Spreads from Mixtec heartland to Cholula, Gulf Coast, and beyond; found in Aztec tribute
- **Form:** Tripod bowls, wide-mouth jars, large plates
- **Colors:** Cream/buff ground; black outline; red, orange, turquoise, yellow fills
- **Imagery:** Deities (Quetzalcoatl, Tlaloc, Xipe), day signs, feathers, skulls; codex-quality painting
- **Cholula Polychrome:** Finest quality; Cholula was the primary production center

*Generative notes:* Same color palette as codex painting but on curved ceramic surface; use the Mixtec codex color system (cream/tan body, red/black/turquoise); figural scenes in a narrow band around vessel

### Tlaloc-Image Vessels
- **Form:** Large ceramic jars/censers with Tlaloc face modeled in the front
- **Diagnostic:** Goggle eyes, fanged jaw, ringed mouth; sometimes painted blue
- **Turquoise Mosaic Censers:** Some elite examples have turquoise or other stone inlay on ceramic base

## Maya Ceramics

### Classic Period Polychrome (400–900 CE)
The Maya Classic ceramic tradition includes some of the finest painted ceramics in world art history.

**Codex-Style Ceramics (800–1000 CE)**
- **Form:** Cylindrical vases (K-vessels); tall, thin-walled
- **Surface:** Cream/buff ground; pure black-and-red imagery; no other colors
- **Imagery:** Scenes from Maya creation myth (Popol Vuh precursors); Hun Hunahpu in the underworld; jaguar lords; ball game
- **The "Primary Standard Sequence" (PSS):** A band of hieroglyphs around the rim; a dedicatory/ownership text standard to many Classic Maya vessels
- **Style:** Black-outline, flat red fills; sophisticated negative space; asymmetric narrative scenes

*Generative notes:* Cream ground (#F2E8C6); black outlines; red fills; tall cylinder vase format; PSS glyph band around rim (use Maya glyph block row); scene panel below the glyph band; underworld lords, maize deity, or ball game scenes

**Polychrome Palace-Style Vessels (600–900 CE)**
- **Form:** Cylindrical vases; some wide bowl forms
- **Colors:** Orange, red, cream, black, brown; occasionally Maya Blue
- **Imagery:** Courtly scenes; seated lords receiving tribute; dancing figures; deity processions; war captives
- **Regional variants:** Chama polychrome (rich warm palette); Holmul dancer vases; Uaxactún polychrome

*Generative notes:* Orange-warm palette; figures in procession or audience scenes; all figures in same horizontal register band; feet touching a baseline; detailed costume; PSS glyph band at top and bottom

**Plumbate Ware (Terminal Classic)**
- **Form:** Effigy vessels, animal forms, human faces; globular bodies
- **Surface:** Distinctive grey-black to olive-grey fired glaze-like surface; high sheen
- **No painting:** The surface quality is the decoration; slip fires to unique vitrified appearance

*Generative notes:* Grey-olive glossy surface (#4A5040); smooth; slightly metallic sheen; sculptural effigy forms (animal heads, deity faces) as vessel attachments

**Fine Orange Ware**
- **Form:** Thin-walled bowls and vases; fine paste (no tempering)
- **Surface:** Smooth orange surface; some incised decoration; some with molded low-relief
- **Distribution:** Trade good across wide area in Terminal Classic

### Incensarios (Censers/Braziers)
- **Form:** Large cylindrical or flanged censers; decorated with modeled deity face and headdress attachments
- **Style:** Multiple flanged disks stacked (like a tiered skirt); large deity mask in front; highly three-dimensional
- **Colors:** Painted with blue, red, white after firing (post-fire paint on buff ceramic)

## Teotihuacan Ceramics

### Thin Orange Ware
- **Period:** ~150–600 CE; widely traded
- **Form:** Tripod cylinder vases, hemispherical bowls, figurines
- **Surface:** Thin, smooth, uniform orange (low-iron local paste); minimal decoration
- **Decoration on vases:** Incised geometric patterns; or stucco-painted after firing

### Tripod Cylinder Vase (Signature Form)
- **Form:** Tall cylindrical vase with three slab (flat vertical) feet and flat lid with ring handle
- **Surface:** Usually grey-buff paste; exterior often covered in lime plaster (stucco) and painted
- **Painted decoration:** Same program as wall murals — horizontal registers of deity faces, geometric bands; Tlaloc masks; net pattern fills
- **Distribution:** Exported throughout Mesoamerica; found at distant Maya sites

*Generative notes:* Cylinder form with three flat rectangular feet; slab lid with ring handle on top; flat surface ideal for painted register band decoration; grey-buff body color under any stucco layer

### Candeleros
- **Form:** Small double-chambered object (like a figure-8 in plan); 5–10 cm; very common
- **Function:** Incense burner; small brazier
- **Decoration:** None; purely utilitarian; clay color (#B8A88A)

## Zapotec Ceramics

### Funerary Urns (see also cultures/zapotec.md)
- **Form:** Cylindrical olla (jar) with full frontal effigy figure or mask attached to the front face
- **Surface:** Natural fired clay; grey-buff; minimal slip; often rough texture at headdress details
- **Construction:** Body formed separately from headdress; headdress assembled from multiple sub-elements; all attached with clay slip before final firing
- **Deity types:** Cocijo, Bat God, Corn God, various lord/lady figures
- **Scale:** 20–80 cm tall; range from simple mask-on-jar to elaborate towering figures

*Generative notes:* Grey-buff (#B8A88A) ceramic body; rigidly frontal effigy face; headdress is a vertical stack extending well above the face; clean fired surface with tactile clay texture

### Grey Ware Bowls
- **Surface:** Reduction-fired grey; incised or carved geometric decoration; high-fired
- **Patterns:** Step-fret border bands; diagonal incision; occasionally figural incision
- **Quality:** Among the finest-made pre-Columbian ceramics for technical execution

## Gulf Coast (Veracruz) Ceramics

### Smiling Figurines (Las Remojadas Tradition)
- **Period:** ~200–900 CE
- **Form:** Hollow figurines; articulated limbs; exaggerated wide-open smile
- **Surface:** Cream or buff; some coated with tar/bitumen (chapopote) as black costume detail
- **Function:** Ritual/funerary; some double as whistles or rattles
- **Style:** Simple, joyful; totally different emotional register from austere Aztec/Zapotec figurines

*Generative notes:* Warm clay tone (#D4906A); exaggerated open-mouthed smile; hollow body; raised arms; feel playful/festive; black bitumen costume marks in selected areas

### Classic Veracruz Fine Paste
- **Surface:** Smooth grey to buff
- **Decoration:** Incised or carved scroll patterns (same interlocking scroll as architecture)
- **Forms:** Cylindrical jars, bowls, effigy forms (sometimes ball-game yoke form in miniature)

## Mixtec Ceramics

### Mixtec Polychrome (Codex-Style Pottery)
- **Form:** Tripod bowls, globular jars, plates with flaring rim
- **Surface:** Cream/buff base slip; orange, red, black painted decoration
- **Imagery:** Same iconographic program as Mixtec codices — deities, day signs, geometric borders
- **Quality:** Some examples are of codex-equivalent quality

### Cholula Polychrome (related Mixteca-Puebla style)
- **Form:** Wide flat plates, hemispherical bowls, effigy forms
- **Colors:** Rich palette: orange, red, turquoise, yellow, black on cream
- **Imagery:** Mixture of Aztec and Mixtec-style deity and geometric imagery

## Generative AI Directives

- **Maya cylinder vase:** Always tall (height > diameter); PSS glyph band around top; narrative scene below in horizontal register; cream/orange ground
- **Aztec black-on-orange:** High-contrast; geometric step-fret dominant; no narrative figuration in painted bands
- **Teotihuacan tripod vase:** Grey-buff cylinder body; three flat slab feet; stucco-painted exterior (same as mural program)
- **Zapotec funerary urn:** Grey-buff clay; rigidly symmetrical frontal effigy; headdress dominates
- **Smiling figurine:** Small hollow clay body; exaggerated joyful expression; arms raised; minimal costume detail
- **Surface rendering:** Ceramics are never perfectly smooth — show slight texture, micro-cracks at thick-thin junctions, and glaze/slip variation
- **Firing atmosphere:** Reduction-fired wares (grey, black) vs. oxidation-fired wares (orange, red, buff); this determines the color of the clay body
