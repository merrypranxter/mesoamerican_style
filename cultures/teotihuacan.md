# Teotihuacan Visual Culture Reference

**Period:** ~100 BCE – 650 CE (florescence 200–550 CE)  
**Region:** Basin of Mexico, ~40 km northeast of modern Mexico City  
**Sites:** Teotihuacan (main city — largest in the Americas at its peak)

---

## Overview for Generative AI

Teotihuacan is the great anonymous empire of ancient Mesoamerica — its rulers are unknown, its language unidentified, but its visual system is among the most sophisticated and geometrically rigorous of any ancient culture. Teotihuacan art is defined by **grid-based composition, flat bold color fills, strong outline conventions, and a unique synthesis of supernatural imagery** that influenced the entire subsequent Mesoamerican visual tradition. It is an art of **system over individual**, of **repeating module over unique masterwork**.

---

## Core Visual Characteristics

- **Grid and register organization** — paintings composed in strict horizontal registers; interior spaces of figures organized by modular grid cells
- **Frontal presentation** — figures and supernatural beings shown frontally, not in profile; creates hieratic, iconic, impersonal quality
- **Flat color fills** — bright, opaque, flat mineral pigment; no shading or modeling; hard edges
- **Thick outlines** — black contour lines of consistent weight defining all forms
- **Tassel headdress** — the most diagnostic headdress type; hanging tassels over forehead; feathers arranged laterally
- **Net jaguar** — jaguar rendered with net pattern on body; distinctive Teotihuacan hybrid
- **Goggle-eyed storm god (Tlaloc)** — the dominant deity; rain/water/lightning; goggle eyes (circles around eyes), fanged jaw; rain drops falling
- **Feathered Serpent (Quetzalcoatl)** — serpent with feathered collar; early emergence of the long-running Mesoamerican deity
- **Butterfly/nose pendant** — butterfly motif frequently worn as nose ornament or combined with military symbols
- **Shell/marine symbols** — conch shells, crossed hands motif; Tlaloc's dominion includes sea imagery

## Key Motifs

### Geometric / Structural
- **Talud-tablero** — the defining architectural profile: alternating sloped base (talud) and vertical panel (tablero); generates a distinctive step silhouette; each tablero is a framed panel for decoration
- **Net/mesh pattern** — grid or diamond net pattern; used as body fill for jaguars, net designs on textiles/border bands
- **Four-petal flower (tetragonal)** — four-petal form; used as central medallion in floor pavements and textile borders
- **Crossed trapezoids / stepped diamonds** — repeated geometric fill pattern; like a flattened diamond-net; used in border bands
- **Year sign** — a rectangle with trapezoidal notch at top or side; derived from a tied bundle; signals year count
- **Frieze band** — continuous horizontal decorative register; most walls are organized as stacked frieze bands

### Supernatural / Symbolic
- **Tlaloc goggle eye** — circular eye ring (sometimes with concentric circles); primary identifier of rain deity faces
- **Water droplet/chalchihuitl** — water drop form; round with tail; infills in rain imagery
- **Storm mountain** — a stepped mountain form with rain dots falling; combines storm + earth
- **Feathered jaguar** — jaguar body with feathers replacing or supplementing spots; sky-earth hybrid
- **Maize footstep path** — footprints in a diagonal path with maize/plant motifs; ritual journey imagery
- **Coyote warrior** — warrior figures in coyote/canine suit with military symbols; warrior-coyote class

### Figurative Conventions
- **Profile arms, frontal body** — unique hybrid: body is strictly frontal, arms shown in profile/action pose
- **Glyph-like hands** — hands in codex-like gesture positions; each carrying distinct implements (incense bag, shield, blowing conch)
- **Speech scroll with water droplets** — speech scrolls filled with liquid/water motif; characteristic of blessing/rain rituals
- **Bloodletting drops** — drops falling from hands or implements during ritual contexts

## Writing and Glyph System

- **Type:** Proto-writing / pictographic notation; not a full phonetic system
- **Year signs:** Rectangular forms with a trapezoid notch; combined with symbols to mark time intervals
- **Name/title glyphs:** Pictographic identifiers attached to figures; similar to Aztec name-glyphs (phonetic rebuses possible)
- **Speech scrolls:** Curved volutes from mouths; filled with water drops or symbols indicating type of speech/action
- **No confirmed decipherment** — unlike Maya, no phonetic script identified; but consistent symbol vocabulary
- **Iconographic grammar:** Symbols read as attributes (what a figure carries/wears identifies its type); more visual-attribute-based than sequential text

## Architecture and Pattern

### City Layout
- **Street of the Dead axis** — 2.4 km north-south main axis; slightly west of true north (15.5°)
- **Grid city plan** — entire 20 km² city organized on this grid; most rigidly planned ancient city in Mesoamerica
- **District clustering** — apartment compounds (residential units), plazas, marketplaces arranged in grid blocks
- **Platform plaza complexes** — each major compound has a sunken rectangular plaza with central altar; four stairways on four sides of altar

### Pyramids
- **Pyramid of the Sun** — ~65 m high; five-stepped; oriented toward solar zenith passage and pleiades setting; talud-tablero exterior
- **Pyramid of the Moon** — at north axis terminus; progressive-stage construction; similar profiles
- **Feathered Serpent Pyramid (Temple of Quetzalcoatl)** — inside the Ciudadela; facade covered with alternating feathered serpent heads and Tlaloc mask heads in horizontal registers; undulating serpent bodies between heads

### Apartment Compounds
- **Standard unit:** Walled rectangular compound; interior rooms arranged around central patio with altar; rooftop and wall mural painting
- **Mural programs:** Systematic painted programs on interior and exterior walls; same system repeated across multiple compounds
- **Tepantitla, Teopanzolco, Tetitla** — famous mural complexes; Tlaloc rain paradise (Tlalocan) mural at Tepantitla

### Decorative Architectural System
- **Tablero frame** — each talud-tablero tablero is a framed panel; the frame itself is a recessed shadow band (molding)
- **Serpent body frieze** — Feathered Serpent Pyramid's body register: undulating green-feathered serpent body running between deity heads
- **Conch shell / spiral** — architectural inset decorative element; snail and conch shells incised or modeled into walls

## Mural Painting Tradition

- **Tepantitla murals (Tlalocan)** — rain paradise; Tlaloc flanked by human figures in procession; background filled with water drops, butterflies, flowers, plants; humans playing, bathing, speaking
- **Technique:** Fresco secco (dry plaster); pigment applied to dried lime plaster
- **Compositional organization:** Always in horizontal registers; upper registers = deities; lower = humans/animals
- **Repeat module:** A repeated figure motif is the building block — individual figure + attribute set repeated across the wall
- **Background fill** — no empty space; background filled with water drops, speech scrolls, small symbols
- **Geometric border bands** — every mural framed by geometric step-fret or net-pattern border bands

---

## Ceramics

- **Thin Orange ware** — widely traded; distinctive thin walls; uniform orange color; minimal decoration; vessel forms include tripod cylinder vases
- **Tripod cylindrical vessels** — the signature Teotihuacan ceramic form; three slab feet; cylindrical body; lid with ring handle; often with stucco-painted exterior scenes
- **Candeleros (candle holders)** — small double-chambered ceramic objects; abundant; used for burning incense; no decoration; purely functional
- **San Martin Orange ceramics** — local domestic ware; hemispherical bowls
- **Stucco-painted decoration** — applied to fired vessel surface; allows polychrome painting similar to wall murals; scenes of deity faces, geometric bands

## Influence Legacy

- **Talud-tablero** — adopted at Monte Albán, Cholula, Kaminaljuyu (Guatemala), and many other sites
- **Goggle-eye deity (Tlaloc)** — spreads as far as the Maya highlands and Gulf Coast
- **Feathered Serpent** — becomes Quetzalcoatl in Aztec tradition; K'uk'ulkan in Maya
- **Apartment compound design** — adopted by Tula, Oaxaca, and later urban centers
- **Tripod cylinder vase** — traded throughout Mesoamerica; appears at distant Maya sites

## Generative AI Directives

- Teotihuacan compositions are **strict horizontal registers** — no diagonal arrangements or scattered layouts
- Figures are **frontally oriented** with flat color fills; no cast shadows or volumetric shading
- **Background fill** is non-negotiable in murals: water drops, small flowers, small glyphs fill every gap
- Goggle eyes = two concentric circles for eyes; **never almond/slit eyes** in Tlaloc imagery
- The **talud-tablero profile** is the primary architectural element: alternating diagonal-ramp and vertical-panel stacking
- Use **deep red ground** (#B83A2E) as the default background color for mural-style compositions
- **Net/mesh pattern** body fill for jaguars; **feathers** as chevron-pattern collar for Feathered Serpent
- Border bands: **step-fret or net-diamond pattern**; consistent width; always parallel to edge
