# Mesoamerican Architecture Reference

## Overview

Mesoamerican architecture shares a common logic across cultures: **elevation of sacred space above the profane**, **solar and astronomical alignment**, **cyclical burial and rebuilding** of structures (each new construction encloses an older one), and **bilateral symmetry** in both plan and elevation. Despite these common principles, each culture produced distinctively different architectural vocabularies.

---

## Universal Structural Principles

### Pyramidal Platform (Teocalli / Temple Mound)
- Structures are **stepped pyramid platforms** rather than solid masses
- Each step is a distinct platform level; the temple or shrine is the final upper element
- **Symbolic meaning:** The pyramid is a mountain (tepetl); the temple at top is a cave in the mountain; the combination = cave in the world mountain = axis of the cosmos
- **Structural method:** Rubble-fill core with stone facing; each new construction buries the previous structure entirely
- **Stairway placement:** Usually on the west-facing side (sunset/underworld access); sometimes on multiple faces (four-stairway pyramids)

### Astronomical Alignment
- Sites are consistently oriented to significant solar events: solstice sunrise/sunset, equinox, Pleiades rising, Venus
- Most common: 8° deviation from magnetic north (corresponding to astronomical north or Pleiades)
- Individual buildings may also be oriented to catch light through specific apertures at specific calendar dates

### Talud-Tablero (Slope-Panel)
- The most widespread architectural profile in Mesoamerica; originated at Teotihuacan
- **Talud:** Inwardly sloping lower wall section
- **Tablero:** Vertical rectangular panel above the talud; often framed with molding and decorated
- Proportions vary: Teotihuacan has wide tablero; Aztec variants have taller talud; Maya variants rarer but present
- Creates a visual rhythm of alternating diagonal/vertical reading up the pyramid face

### Twin Temples
- Aztec twin-stair pyramids (Templo Mayor model): two parallel temples at summit with dual stairways
- Symbolizes duality: one temple for sun/war deity (Huitzilopochtli), one for rain/water deity (Tlaloc)
- Visible as a distinctive "W" silhouette from the front

## Culture-Specific Architectural Vocabularies

### Aztec (Mexica) Architecture

**Templo Mayor (Tenochtitlan)**
- Double-stairway pyramid with twin shrines at summit
- Red shrine (Huitzilopochtli/Sun) on the south; blue shrine (Tlaloc/Rain) on the north
- Seven phases of construction; each new phase encases the previous
- Decorated with serpent heads (stone) on the terrace platforms
- Skull rack (Tzompantli) platform adjacent; carved skulls in rows

**Decorative System**
- Serpent balustrades: feathered serpent heads at the base of stairway balustrades; bodies undulate up the sides
- Platform face decoration: step-fret (xicalcoliuhqui) carved into talud-tablero faces
- Corner protections: guardian figures or serpent heads at terrace corners
- Polychrome: originally painted red (south) and blue (north) with white and other pigments

**Profile Silhouette**
- Steep, aggressive; many small steps; strong horizontal shadow lines at each terrace
- Viewing angle from below looking up emphasizes height and power

### Maya Architecture — Regional Styles

**Petén (Tikal, Uaxactun, ~300–900 CE)**
- Towering steep pyramids with very small, narrow summit temples
- Massive roof combs — tall stone latticework rising from temple roof; increases visual height to 3–4× temple body
- Apron moldings at base and summit of temple
- Corbeled vault interiors: tall narrow rooms with inward-stepped stone courses forming pointed vault

*Generative notes:* Extreme vertical emphasis; narrow temple with huge roof comb; dense tropical-overgrown context; stucco facade relief visible on lower building faces

**Palenque (Chiapas, ~600–800 CE)**
- Lower, more horizontal; elegant proportions; mansard-profile roof combs (leaning back)
- Piers with elaborate stucco relief figures between them
- Cross-shaped T-shaped vault with clerestory (lattice opening to let light/air through roof comb)
- Interior rooms with multiple chambers and doors
- Aqueduct system for water management

*Generative notes:* Elegant, refined; roof combs are open lattice not solid mass; piers between doorways carry figural stucco relief; warm red-orange stucco on faces; jungle-wrapped setting

**Puuc Style (Uxmal, Kabah, Sayil, ~700–1000 CE)**
- Smooth undecorated lower zone (from ground to medial molding)
- Elaborate upper zone: dense stone mosaic of geometric patterns, step-fret bands, and stacked deity masks
- Chac mask stacks at corners: profile rain deity masks assembled from modular stone elements, stacked 3–7 high
- Colonnettes (round mini-columns) as decorative vertical elements in upper zone
- Rounded columns flanking doorways

*Generative notes:* Strong horizontal two-zone composition; bottom half plain stone, top half dense mosaic; corner stacks of Chac masks are vertical accent elements; cooler grey stone tones; Yucatan dry-tropical context

**Chichen Itza / Toltec-Maya (~900–1200 CE)**
- El Castillo (Kukulkan Pyramid): nine platforms, four stairways, exactly 91 steps each (365 total), serpent shadow effect at equinox
- Colonnaded halls (Temple of Warriors, Group of 1000 Columns)
- Great Ball Court: massive I-shape, vertical wall rings, skull panels on benches
- Round tower observatory (El Caracol)

*Generative notes:* Grand scale; warrior column forests; circular El Caracol stands out as unique round form; serpent balustrades with flat feathered serpent heads at stairway base

### Teotihuacan Architecture

**City Plan**
- 2.4 km Street of the Dead axis; entire city laid out on a grid offset 15.5° from north
- Sunken plaza and altar as standard building unit: rectangular plaza with altar platform at center; entered through a restricted gateway
- Apartment compounds: residential units of ~60 m × 60 m; inward-facing rooms around central patio with altar

**Pyramids**
- Pyramid of the Sun: five-tiered; broadest base (223 m × 225 m); original flat summit (destroyed); stairway on western face
- Pyramid of the Moon: at north end of Street of the Dead; similar but smaller; successive construction phases
- Feathered Serpent Pyramid (Ciudadela): most elaborately decorated; alternating feathered serpent + storm deity heads in horizontal registers on all pyramid faces

**Decorative System**
- Talud-tablero: standard profile; tablero is the decorated zone
- Tablero decoration: carved/modeled relief within the tablero frame; at Feathered Serpent Pyramid, projecting stone heads (tenons) alternate between two deity types at each tablero register
- Mural programs: interior walls painted with horizontal register mural programs (see Teotihuacan culture reference)

---

### Zapotec Architecture (Monte Albán)

**Site Plan**
- Summit of mountain leveled to create grand plaza (~600 × 300 m)
- Platforms line the north and south ends; subsidiary platforms on east and west
- Central platform in the middle of the plaza
- Building J (arrowhead form): angled differently from the rest; possible astronomical observatory

**Platform Profile**
- Distinctive "Zapotec talud-tablero": a scapular (shoulder-like) molding divides wall into lower and upper sections
- Upper wall is vertical; lower wall slopes outward slightly; separated by a narrow recessed "neckline" channel
- Stairways with wide alfardas (apron/side walls)

**Tomb Architecture**
- Elaborate underground tombs carved from bedrock; stone-lined; corbeled or flat-roofed
- Cruciform (cross-shaped) plan for important tombs
- Entry vestibule, main chamber, side niches
- Interior walls painted with figural and geometric murals (Lambityeco, Yagul)
- Zapotec urns placed flanking entry vestibule

---

### Gulf Coast / Tajín Architecture

**Pyramid of the Niches**
- Seven tiers; 25 m high
- Each tier face subdivided into equally spaced rectangular niches (365 total)
- Each niche creates a shadow square; the entire pyramid surface is a pattern of shadow-squares
- Stairway on east face; flying cornices project above each step

*Generative notes:* The niches are the visual identity — use them as a texture/pattern. Rectangular recesses in a pyramidal arrangement. Niche depth creates strong shadow geometry that changes throughout the day. Use for any Veracruz-inspired composition.

## Cross-Cultural Decorative Patterns

### Border Band Vocabulary
These patterns appear as horizontal bands framing compositions, temple tiers, and doorway moldings:

| Pattern              | Culture(s)        | Description                                              | Generative Notes                                |
|----------------------|-------------------|----------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| Step-fret meander    | Pan-Mesoamerican  | Right-angle spiral stair pattern; key/Greek-key variant  | Alternating horizontal+vertical L-turns; mirror |
| Xicalcoliuhqui       | Aztec             | Scrolled stepped spiral; a curved step-fret              | L-turn + U-curve combined; slightly rounded     |
| Interlocking scroll  | Veracruz/Tajín    | Paired S-curves interlocking continuously                | Infinite tiling of S/C curve pairs              |
| Running water scroll | Maya, Teotihuacan | Wave with curl at top; repeating undulation              | Sinusoidal with curl; velocity implied          |
| Skull row            | Aztec             | Repeating skull faces in horizontal register             | Equal-spaced frontal skulls; some with crossed bones below |
| Serpent procession   | Toltec, Aztec     | Profile rattlesnakes at equal intervals                  | Head → body undulation → rattle, repeat         |
| Chalchihuitl strip   | Aztec             | Row of chalchihuitl (jade/water) symbols                 | Circle with 4-dot surround, repeat              |
| Sky/cauac band       | Maya              | Celestial band with sky-symbol sequence                  | Alternating star, kin, akbal, imix cartouches   |
| Trefoil border       | Olmec             | Rounded triple-lobed border element                      | Three adjacent semicircles; fluid organic form  |

### Doorway / Threshold Conventions
- **Maya temple doorway:** Tall narrow corbeled opening; jambs (sides) often carved with standing figures; lintel above carries inscriptions and scenes
- **Aztec doorway:** Trapezoidal opening with sloped sides (wider at base); serpent or deity head framing elements
- **Teotihuacan doorway:** Simple rectangular; flanked by pilasters; part of a colonnaded facade

### Stairway Conventions
- **Aztec balustrade (alfarda):** Decorated side-wall of stairway; feathered serpent undulates along it; serpent head at bottom
- **Maya stairway:** Hieroglyphic stairway (Copan); each riser-face carved with glyphs; continuous text running up the stairs
- **Zapotec stairway:** Wide alfardas with scapular profile matching building walls

## Generative AI Directives

- **No generic pyramid** — always specify the culture and the distinctive profile: twin-stair (Aztec), steep-tower (Maya Petén), talud-tablero (Teotihuacan), niche-face (Tajín), scapular-profile (Zapotec)
- **Solar alignment is always present** — in any architectural scene, indicate the sun angle corresponding to a meaningful astronomical event
- **Border bands should be culture-specific** — step-fret for Aztec/Zapotec; interlocking scroll for Veracruz; stacked Chac for Puuc Maya; running water for Teotihuacan
- **Always include the figure-to-architecture scale** — Mesoamerican buildings are built to be seen from a distance; individual figures appear small against pyramid faces
- **Interior spaces** = narrow, dark, mysterious (corbeled vaults, torch-lit); these are inverse of the broad exterior plazas
- **Weathering** = most surfaces are worn; lime plaster peeling; original pigment partially surviving in protected recesses; stone surface has patina
